# VOLUME I: The Go and the Gu
*(Go — the game of stones / Gu — the name of a king)*

*There is a kind of intelligence*  
*that does not announce itself.*  
*It simply watches.*  
*And waits.*  
*And already knows the ending*  
*before you have even decided to begin.*

---

### **Part I. Treasure in the Path**
There are mornings in Hanyang that smell of river fog and cold charcoal. On those mornings, the lower market opens the way all tired things do—slowly, without ceremony, simply because stopping is not an option.

Nam Jung Hae was twelve years old on one such morning.

He sat at a corner of the market where the foot traffic thinned—not at the center where the noise was deafening and coins flowed freely, but where old grain sacks were piled and no merchant bothered to claim the space. He had learned, through two years of this life, that the corner was better. People who wandered to the edge were curious. Curious people stayed longer. Staying longer meant more stones were placed. More stones placed meant more meals.

He laid out the Go board on a flattened grain sack and waited.

He did not look hungry, though he was. He did not look lonely, though the word had long since lost its meaning to him. To those who noticed him at all, he looked like a boy who was simply and completely at home in the world he occupied.

That was no accident.

Jung Hae had learned early on that people do not give coins to desperation; they give coins to competence. So he sat straight. He reset the stones with clean, unhurried movements. He kept his eyes on the board rather than the crowd—the posture of someone who does not need you. People, he found, were always more drawn to those who did not need them.

He was twelve years old. He had figured this out at ten.

The first man to sit down that morning was a merchant's assistant—young, bored, and possessing the particular confidence of someone who had once been told he was clever. He placed a bronze mun on the edge of the board without looking at Jung Hae's face.

"I'll play you," the man said. "If you win, keep the coin. If I win, I take the board."

Jung Hae looked at the coin, then at the board, then at the assistant's hands—the way they rested on his knees, the angle of his fingers, and the absence of any calluses from stone-work. This man had never played a serious game in his life.

"One coin is not worth this board," Jung Hae said.

"Then name your terms."

"You play. If I win, three coins. If you win, the board and your coin back."

The assistant agreed. Young men with borrowed confidence rarely back down in public, and Jung Hae had known he would agree before the sentence was even finished.

He lost the first game on purpose—not obviously or insultingly, but in a way that left the man feeling he had almost achieved something. A near-miss. A "could-have-been." The assistant leaned forward by the third move. By the seventh, he was talking aloud. By the fifteenth, he was completely absorbed in a version of the game that Jung Hae had already decided the shape of.

Jung Hae won the second game in eleven moves.

The assistant left three coins on the board's edge—one more than agreed. It was a gesture of combined shame and generosity. Jung Hae had seen it before. He put all three coins in the cloth pouch at his belt and began resetting the stones, already watching the crowd for his next opponent.

### **Part II. Nam Jung Hae**
The man in the grey robe appeared during the fourth hour.

Jung Hae noticed him the way he noticed most things—peripherally, without turning his head. The man had been standing at the corner of the textile stall across the way for perhaps twenty minutes. He was not shopping, nor was he waiting for anyone. He was watching the Go board.

He was not watching Jung Hae. He was watching the board.

That was different. Most people watched Jung Hae—the small, straight-backed boy with the too-serious face and rope-tied sandals. They watched him because he was an oddity. This man watched the board the way one watches something they already understand and are deciding what to do with.

That was interesting.

When the man finally crossed over and sat down, Jung Hae read him in the space of three breaths.

He was fifty, perhaps a little older. He was lean through the jaw and temples. His hands were unmarked by physical labor—he was neither merchant, nor soldier, nor craftsman. His robe was plain in color, but the weave was dense and fine—the kind of plainness that costs more than decoration. The way he settled himself carried a particular quality: not just comfort, but control. It was the deliberate arrangement of a man who was always aware of how he occupied space.

And his eyes—when they finally moved from the board to Jung Hae's face, they held the quality of assessment. They were precise and unhurried—the kind of gaze that collects information and stores it without reaction.

Jung Hae had seen that gaze before on magistrates—men who decided the fates of others.

A high official of some kind, he thought. Someone with authority. Someone used to rooms where people are careful around him.

He filed this away and waited. The man did not place a coin. He simply sat, settled his robes, and said—without introduction:

"Your corner territory is under-protected."

"I know," Jung Hae replied.

"Then why leave it?"

"Because the man I was playing never looks at corners. His type looks only at the center; they think control is found in the middle." Jung Hae set a stone down quietly. "So I give them the middle and I take everything around it."

The man was still for a moment. "You read your opponents."

"I read everyone," Jung Hae said simply. "It is faster than reading the board."

The man looked at him fully, in a way adults rarely looked at children in the lower market. "What is your name?"

"Nam Jung Hae."

"Nam." The man repeated the surname steadily, without reaction. Then, "My name is Gu."

Jung Hae nodded once. It was a receipt of information, not a conversation. The man reached out and placed the first black stone of a new game.

Jung Hae looked at where it landed, then at the man's face, then back at the stone. He picked up a white stone and placed it. And quietly, without showing it, he began to read.

### **Part III. Lost**
The first game, Jung Hae lost.

He lost genuinely—not through carelessness or design. The man across from him played in a way Jung Hae had never encountered, and the unfamiliarity of it was like trying to read a sentence in a language he almost knew. He understood some of the words, but not the grammar. Not yet.

But he was learning it in real-time.

Every stone the man placed was a sentence. Jung Hae read each one—not just for its function on the board, but for what it revealed about the mind behind it. He saw the patience and the economy. He saw how sacrifice was used not as an act of desperation, but as architecture—building something with the spaces left behind rather than the stones placed forward.

By the twelfth move, Jung Hae understood the basic structure of the language. By the eighteenth, he had identified the grammar.

He lost the game, but he felt as a student does upon finishing a lesson—not defeated, but fulfilled. He sat quietly afterward, turning a white stone over in his fingers, contemplating what he had just been given.

The man placed a silver yang on the board's edge when the game ended.

"For the game," he said.

"You won," Jung Hae noted.

"Yes. But you played honestly." The man rose and adjusted his robe. "I will return tomorrow."

He left without waiting for an answer. Jung Hae watched him go—noticing how the crowd parted for him without touching him, and how even strangers made space without being asked.

He thought: This man is used to being the most important person in any room.

He thought: I wonder what he wants from me.

He thought: I will know by the end of the week.

He picked up the silver yang and reset the stones.

### **Part IV. The Winning Streak**
The man returned the next morning. And the morning after that. And every morning for three weeks.

He was always in the grey robe. Always without introduction. Always using a different method.

That was the first thing Jung Hae noticed—and the most important. Every morning the man arrived and played differently. Not randomly, but deliberately. It was as if each method were a separate test, a distinct question posed through the language of stones.

One morning he played aggressively, pushing from the first move and claiming territory with the confidence of one who expects to be obeyed. The next morning he played passively—almost lazily—letting Jung Hae expand freely and giving ground with the ease of someone who does not fear losing space because they intend to reclaim it in a single move later. The morning after that, he played with military precision—the same sacrificial architecture and collapse-from-within he had used on the first day.

Then came something new: a style Jung Hae had no name for, built entirely on misdirection. Here, the apparent threat was never the real threat; every stone that looked like an attack was, in truth, a question about a move three steps ahead.

Every morning brought a different language.

Jung Hae lost the first four games. He lost them as he had lost the very first—honestly, but not emptily. Each loss was a lesson, and he treated it as such: dismantling the game in his memory, rebuilding it stone by stone, and finding the seam where his understanding had reached its limit.

By the fifth morning, he stopped losing.

It was not because he had mastered the man's methods, but because he had mastered something more fundamental: he had learned how the man thought. He understood the architecture of the mind behind the stones—the values it held, the patterns it preferred, and the way it responded to pressure, openness, and the unexpected.

When you understand how a mind thinks, you do not need to know its method. You already know what it will do before it does it.

Jung Hae began to win.

He was careful about this. He won narrowly at first—by one point, then two, then four. He did not sweep the board or dismantle his opponent. He let the games feel close. He felt the man lean forward slightly when the outcome was uncertain, his attention sharpening. Jung Hae watched this and understood: the man was not just playing Go. He was measuring something. And whatever he was measuring, he needed to believe the measurement was his to take.

So Jung Hae let him believe it. He played well enough to win, but not well enough to alarm.

He was twelve years old. He had been doing this since he was ten.

On the morning of the third week, the man said, "You have improved remarkably."

"I had a good teacher," Jung Hae replied.

"I have not taught you anything."

"No," Jung Hae agreed. "But you showed me everything."

The man looked at him with that precise, collecting gaze. "What did I show you?"

Jung Hae set a stone down, looked at the board, then at the man. "How you think. That is more useful than any method."

The man was silent for a long moment. Then he placed his stone, and they played.

Jung Hae won that game by six points—his most decisive margin yet. He felt the man sitting very still afterward. It wasn't the stillness of defeat; it was the stillness of a person arriving at an answer they had been calculating for a very long time.

He thought: Whatever he came here for, he has decided.

He thought: Something is going to happen soon.

He reset the stones and waited.

### **Part V. Manipulation**
The morning of the twenty-second day was the final morning.

Jung Hae felt it before the man even sat down. There was a shift in the quality of the silence. The four men at the edges of the alley—those he had been aware of since the second week but had never acknowledged—were positioned differently today. They were closer. More deliberate. The man in the official black robes who had begun appearing three days ago stood nearer than before.

The man in the grey robe arrived and sat, but he did not reach for a stone immediately. He looked at Jung Hae.

"One more game," he said. "Play me as you truly can. No margins. No restraint. Everything."

Jung Hae met his eyes. He thought about what it meant to be asked this, why it was being asked today, and what would follow. Then he reached out and placed the first stone.

"All right," he said.

He played as he truly could.

It was a different thing to witness. Where before he had been careful and measured, now he was simply himself—unconstrained. He did not merely attack or defend. He did something that had no single name: he flowed around the man's every move like water around a stone, finding the exact space between resistance and surrender, shaping the board from the inside out.

He did not play against the man's strategy; he played from within the man's mind.

Every move the man made, Jung Hae saw coming—not because he was guessing, but because he had spent twenty-two mornings studying the grammar of this particular mind until it was no longer foreign. The man played brilliantly, changing his method three times in a single game—a feat Jung Hae had never seen—but it did not matter. Jung Hae was already there, waiting.

He won by thirty-one points. A total collapse of the grey robe's position.

When the final stone was placed, the silence in the alley was so heavy it felt as if the market noise had been cut away by a blade. The men at the edges of the alley had gone perfectly still.

Jung Hae did not look up. He looked at the board. He looked at the truth of what he had done.

"You knew," the man said. His voice was low, devoid of the authority it usually carried.

"I knew," Jung Hae said.

"Since when?"

"The first game I won. I saw how you reacted. You weren't playing to win; you were playing to see if I could be made to lose." Jung Hae finally looked up. His face was as calm as the river fog. "You were testing my ceiling. So I built a floor just below your eyes and let you stand on it."

The man's hands, which had been so controlled for three weeks, tightened slightly on his knees. It was the only sign of the shock he must have felt—the realization that he had been read like a text and had his own mind turned against him for three weeks.

"And the plan?" the man asked. His voice remained steady, still collecting information. "You said it was all your plan from the beginning."

Jung Hae turned a white stone over in his fingers. "I knew by the second morning that you weren't here for Go. A man like you—the way you hold yourself, the way people make space for you—you don't come to the lower market for twenty mornings to play a street child for a silver yang. You came for something. I didn't know what."

"So?"

"So I decided to make myself worth whatever that was. If I lost every game, you would lose interest. If I won too early or too clearly, you might feel threatened. I had to win in the right way, at the right pace. Enough to show you what I was, but not so much that you felt eclipsed."

He looked up. "I don't know what you came for, but I know you've decided. And I know that whatever you are about to offer me, you've been waiting for this morning to do it."

The man stared at him for a long time. Then, he did something Nam Jung Hae would remember for the rest of his life.

He laughed.

It wasn't loud or performative. It was a quiet exhale through the nose, a slight crinkle at the corners of eyes that had likely been still for years. It was the laughter of a man who had spent his life being the most prepared person in the room and had just encountered, for the first time in a very long time, something he had not fully prepared for.

"How old are you?"

"Twelve."

The man shook his head slightly. The laughter faded, but it left something warmer behind—recognition rather than mere assessment. He reached into his grey robe and placed a jade token on the board's edge. It was small and carved—the kind of seal Jung Hae vaguely understood represented official correspondence and matters far beyond the lower market.

"Come with me," the man said. "I will teach you to read. I will teach you to write. I will give you warmth, shelter, and more Go boards than you have ever seen."

"And in return?"

"In return, you think. The way you think now—about people, and the space between what they show and what they mean. But the board will be wider. The people will be armies. The game will be kingdoms."

Jung Hae looked at the jade token, then the board, then the man. He thought about leaving this corner, the grain sacks, and his rope-tied sandals. He had been alone since he was ten. He was hungry most mornings and cold most nights. The monk who taught him Go had been dead for two years. No one would notice if he simply vanished.

He thought of all this, then he thought of the game.

"I have one condition," Nam Jung Hae said.

"Name it."

He gestured to the board. "We finish this first."

"This game is finished," the man said, looking at the thirty-one-point victory.

"I know. I mean a new one. One more game, before I decide."

The man studied him, then swept the stones from the board with one clean motion and began resetting them. "Agreed."

They played the final game. Jung Hae did not try to win, nor did he try to lose. He played fully and honestly, without plan or performance. For the first time in three weeks, there was nothing between them but the game itself. No reading, no architecture of strategy—just two people playing Go on a grain sack in the cold morning.

The man won by three points.

Jung Hae looked at the board for a long time. He found the move—the seventh move, where he had chosen based on instinct rather than calculation, and it had been wrong. He stored that realization away for later.

"All right," he said.

He stood, tucked the board under his arm, and gathered his cloth pouch. He looked at the corner—the grain sacks and the narrow space that had been his for two years—as if memorizing it. Then he turned and fell into step beside the man in the grey robe.

### **Part VI. Adoption**
They walked north through the market and toward streets Jung Hae did not know. The man in the official black robes followed three paces behind. The four silent men rearranged themselves around them, as invisible and omnipresent as water.

The market continued behind them. No one noticed that a boy had walked out of that corner for the last time.

After a while, the man spoke without turning his head. "You said you knew I wasn't here for Go. That you decided to make yourself worth my time."

"Yes."

"Was there anything you did not calculate?"

Jung Hae thought honestly. "The last game. I didn't calculate that. I just played."

"You lost."

"I know."

"Did it bother you?"

Jung Hae considered this. "No. It felt like the most honest game we played."

The man walked in silence for a moment. "It was."

They turned onto a wider street, paved differently, with taller buildings. Jung Hae watched the people, who moved with the careful deference of those who know they are being watched. At the far end of the street, he saw high gates and rooflines that curved upward—the kind he had only seen in paintings.

He thought about the jade token. The way the crowd made space. The four silent men who were more than mere guards. He thought about the man's surname, given so easily: Gu.

He began to rearrange the pieces in his mind: High official. Very important. The highest kind. What is higher than the highest kind of official? he wondered. Where are we walking? What does it mean that his surname is Gu?

He did not speak his thoughts. He was twelve, and he was not yet certain. Certainty was the only good reason to speak.

He kept walking, pressing the board against his ribs. He watched the gates draw closer.

*He would find out the truth that evening.*

*He would be sitting with a brush he did not yet know how to hold, and the man in the dark official robes would say something in passing, almost without weight.*

*The word would land in the middle of Nam Jung Hae's chest like a stone placed on the exact center of a board.*

***"His Majesty."***

*Nam Jung Hae would go completely still. And in the silence of that stillness, he would not feel afraid. He would not feel small.*

*He would think, with the clean precision he had been born with:*

***I manipulated the King of Joseon.***

***I was twelve years old.***

***I did not know.***

*And then, because he was who he was, he would pick up the brush and begin to learn.*
